Should companies track employee health? â€“ YES

Health and well-being as key indicators of productivity.
By Julian Earwaker â€“ based on comments by Kevin Thomson

Employee health and well-being are among the most important assets of any business and key indicators of productivity in the workplace. In the past ten years, organizations have moved away from tick-box surveillance of employees to a broader well-being approach. Around 60 per cent of organizations in the UK now have a health and well-being strategy for their employees.

Health tracking can give an overview of employee health and identify key risks so that interventions can be put in place. In the UK, 1.4 million workers suffer from work-related health problems. From 2017 to 2018, there were hundreds of thousands of new cases of work-related illness and millions of working days lost because of work-related ill health.

Employers have a responsibility to support and empower staff to live healthier and longer lives, particularly as the working population ages and diversifies. Technology has transformed corporate well-being programmes. Personalized health assessments are supported by artificial intelligence to produce an accurate picture of individual employees.

Employees receive secure information about their lifestyle, nutrition and mental health. Employers receive an anonymized summary of that data to help them shape workforce health interventions. Using digital tools, organizations can identify risks and encourage positive changes in behaviour. Most people carry a mobile phone, and tracking devices are available if they want to manage their own health.

There is a risk that monitoring peopleâ€™s health could become a surveillance tool to track employees. Staff should be made aware in advance what their data is going to be used for. Employers should encourage employees through trust, respect and empowerment. Large tech companies that have supported this culture are often the ones doing best in the marketplace.

Businesses sometimes struggle to find platforms that gather health data in one place. It is a logistical challenge, and any platform must meet GDPR (general data protection regulation) requirements. Privacy is a sensitive subject, and staff may be careful about giving out personal information. Employers want to support individuals, but they also want to see the economic returns that may be associated with corporate healthcare programmes.

Argument against tracking

Should companies track employee health? â€“ NO

The risks of misuse and discrimination.
By Julian Earwaker â€“ based on comments by Matt Creagh

There is increasing use of health-related data tracking at work, and rules should be put in place to protect workers. There is great potential for workforce data to be misused. In the workplace, people often feel pressured to consent to sharing their data because they fear there might be negative consequences if they say no.

Employers can create profiles of their workers by collecting a wide range of data. A profile can be used to predict how a worker is going to perform in the future, which could lead to negative inferences. In a worst-case scenario, this could lead to employers discriminating against workers because of their health.

Workforce data can be used to make decisions about disciplinary action, hiring and firing, promotions and pay rises. This is why workers should have a voice in data-driven decision-making processes. The strongest protection workers have is GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation), which gives them legal rights to control how their data is collected, used and stored.

People need to know what their rights are and that these rights will be properly enforced. Technology should be used for the benefit of workers, for example to make sure they are taking adequate breaks away from their desks. Health data could be made anonymous and viewed as a whole across an organization, rather than be connected to individuals.

This would enable an employer to bring well-being benefits to the organization as a whole rather than focus on individual health data. In some countries, data-protection laws are more relaxed and there is evidence that GDPR is not always followed. Working people have a right to a private life, and the law protects that right. An employerâ€™s data collection must not infringe anyoneâ€™s privacy rights.

We are dealing with health-related data, which is obviously very sensitive. Companies and their employees increasingly use technological devices such as Fitbits and sleep-tracking software. If employees use these devices in their spare time, they might not be aware that the data is still being collected and used within a work context.
